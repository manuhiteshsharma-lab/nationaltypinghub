Learn Touch Typing: The Secret to 50+ WPM

Chapter 1: The Setup

Posture & Wrist Placement

Before you touch a single key, you must set up your body. Bad posture leads to slow typing and fatigue. Good posture gives your fingers the freedom to fly across the keys.

🧍 Body & Screen

  • Sit up straight with your back supported and feet flat on the floor.
  • Keep your screen at eye level to avoid neck strain.
  • Keep your elbows bent at a comfortable 90-degree angle.

⚠️ The Wrist Rule

  • Crucial: Keep your wrists hovering slightly above the keyboard or desk.
  • Do not rest your wrists flat on the table. Resting them acts like an anchor, limiting your finger reach and slowing you down.

Chapter 3: The Rules of Movement

Rule 1: The Home Row Base

The middle row of letters is your "Home Base." Before you type anything, your fingers must rest lightly on these exact keys:

Left Hand:

  • Pinky on A
  • Ring on S
  • Middle on D
  • Index on F (Feel the little bump)

Right Hand:

  • Index on J (Feel the little bump)
  • Middle on K
  • Ring on L
  • Pinky on ; (Semicolon)

Both thumbs should hover gently over the Spacebar. The physical bumps on the F and J keys are manufactured there so you can find your Home Base without looking down at your hands.

Rule 2: Reaching & Returning

When you need to type a key on the top row (like R) or the bottom row (like C), you reach up or down with the assigned finger, strike the key, and immediately return that finger to the Home Row.

Your hands should stay stationary over the Home Row. Only your individual fingers stretch up and down.

Rule 3: The Opposite Shift Rule

To type a capital letter, you must use the Shift key. The rule is simple: Always use the Shift key on the opposite side of the keyboard.

  • If you need to type a capital T (which uses your left index finger), hold down the Right Shift key with your right pinky.
  • If you need to type a capital P (which uses your right pinky), hold down the Left Shift key with your left pinky.
